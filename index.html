Origins · 2021

From bare ground

When we began in 2021, there was no garden here at all — only bare ground, the scattered remnants of construction, and a stubborn underground web of blackberry roots that had to be dug out by hand. Everything you've walked through today started from that blank, broken slate.

Two visions for the land

From the start, we imagined the plot in two halves. One half was intended to become a Pacific Northwest meadow — loose, native and seasonal, the soft grassland that belongs so naturally to this corner of the world.

Ultimately, though, our hearts were set on something else: a true Mediterranean garden, like the one at Kassiopia. That vision — sun-loving and drought-adapted, all silver foliage and aromatic scent — is the one that came to shape Sparrowhawk.

The Pacific Northwest meadow

These are the photos of the Pacific Northwest meadow on that half of the garden — a gentle counterpoint to the gravel and silver of the Mediterranean beds.

We created it in collaboration with Northwest Meadowscapes, whose native seed brought the meadow to life.

About Northwest Meadowscapes

Northwest Meadowscapes is a family-owned, ecologist-staffed native-seed company rooted in the Pacific Northwest, supplying grasses and wildflowers for meadows, pollinator habitat and rain gardens across Oregon and Washington. They work only with true, 100% native species — not the cheap, mass-market “wildflower” blends — composed so that something is always coming into flower, in succession across the seasons and from one year to the next.

Their guiding idea, sometimes called meadowscaping, is to replace thirsty turf lawn with living habitat for bees, butterflies, birds and other wildlife. It carries real urgency: native Northwest meadows are among the most biologically diverse landscapes in the region, yet less than one percent of them remain. Having collaborated with pollinator-conservation groups such as the Xerces Society, they help bring a little of that vanishing ecosystem back, one garden at a time.

Learn more at northwestmeadowscapes.com.

Climate

A Mediterranean rhythm at 48° North

Sparrowhawk sits on a south-facing slope on Whidbey Island, just outside Oak Harbor — and tucked inside the Olympic rain shadow. That quirk of geography is what makes a Mediterranean garden possible this far north.

What makes a climate “Mediterranean”

A true Mediterranean climate is defined less by heat than by rhythm: dry summers and mild, wet winters — the opposite of most of the world, where the rain arrives in summer. It is a genuinely rare pattern, found in only five places on Earth: the Mediterranean Basin itself, coastal California, central Chile, the Cape of South Africa, and south-western Australia. Each grows its own version of tough, aromatic, evergreen shrubland — maquis and garrigue in Greece, matorral in Chile, fynbos in South Africa.

Sparrowhawk's microclimate

West of the Cascades, the Pacific Northwest already follows that summer-dry, winter-wet rhythm. But the Olympic Mountains wring most of the rain from the air before it reaches us, leaving Whidbey Island in a rain shadow: Oak Harbor sees only about twenty inches of rain a year — roughly half of Seattle's — with markedly more sun. Add a south-facing slope, which gathers light and warmth and drains freely, and you have a pocket that behaves far more like the dry Mediterranean than the dripping rainforest most people picture when they think of Washington.

The real difference is temperature. At nearly 48° North, our summers are cool rather than scorching and our winters can bite, so Sparrowhawk leans on the hardier end of the Mediterranean palette — lavender, rosemary, cistus, santolina — rather than the most tender species. Think of it as a cool-summer cousin of Greece.

The Greatest Influence

The Gardens at Kassiopia

Of all the places that shaped Sparrowhawk, none looms larger than the gardens of the Kassiopia Estate, set on a rocky limestone headland on the mountainous north-east coast of the island of Corfu, just beyond the fishing village of Kassiopi. More than any single book or border, these gardens are the reason Sparrowhawk looks the way it does.

The setting

Corfu is often called the Garden Isle — a lush, rugged landscape of centuries-old olives and dark, sentinel cypresses cloaking the hillsides. Unusually for Greece, it is among the kindest places in the country to make a garden, blessed with high rainfall, fertile soil, humidity and little wind. The Kassiopia gardens occupy a four-acre headland looking out across the Corfu Straits to the Albanian mountains, snow-capped in winter. House and garden were conceived together, so that the lines between building, planting and sea all but dissolve.

A garden drawn from the wild

What makes Kassiopia so instructive is that the garden was coaxed from the land already there rather than imposed upon it. The first work was not planting but clearing — easing brambles and smilax away from the dense maquis to free smothered Kermes oak, buckthorn and mock privet, and revealing a woodland of winding paths and glades running down to the water. Once light and space returned, so did long-dormant cyclamen, rescued wild pears, drifts of asphodel and a meadow flora studded with wild iris. The natural diversity of the site became the design's guide.

Two climates on one headland

The headland holds two microclimates. Its exposed north-east face catches cold winds off the Albanian mountains, so the plants there must shrug off the occasional freeze; a sheltered south-east corner stays warm enough for tender favourites such as bougainvillea, plumbago, agapanthus and citrus. Reading those differences and planting to them — rather than fighting them — is a lesson Sparrowhawk took straight to heart.

The planting — where Sparrowhawk begins

Nearer the houses, the planting becomes phrygana, the Greek cousin of the French garrigue: low, aromatic, cushion-shaped shrubs threaded by paths of local stone, with olives and pomegranates framing rather than blocking the view. The palette will look familiar to anyone who has walked Sparrowhawk — grey-green cistus and thyme, silver helichrysum and lavender, white-felted Convolvulus cneorum and deep green rosemary, lifted by pastel perennials and clouds of self-seeding Stipa grass that ripple like the sea in the lightest breeze and glow at either end of the day.

Cistus, thyme, helichrysum, lavender, rosemary — nearly every plant in our own beds can be traced back to this Corfiot hillside.

Working with water

For all the island's lushness, Kassiopia still gardens against drought. Rainwater is harvested for the summer, but watering is kept deliberately deep and infrequent, driving roots downward and teaching each plant to fend for itself. As the garden matures, self-seeding lets it renew itself with only a guiding hand. That principle — independence over indulgence — is the quiet rule behind every bed at Sparrowhawk.

Acknowledgements

The inspiration behind Sparrowhawk

This garden owes its spirit to two people who have spent their lives showing what a Mediterranean garden can be when it works with the dry summer rather than against it. Their plants, their writing, and their own gardens shaped every bed you've walked through today.

Nurseryman & Dry-Gardening Pioneer · South of France

Olivier Filippi

Who he is

With his wife Clara, Olivier Filippi runs Pépinière Filippi, a nursery on a windswept site overlooking the lagoons of the Étang du Thau near Mèze, in the south of France. Begun in the 1980s, it has grown into a reference point for Mediterranean horticulture, holding around fifteen hundred species and varieties. Together the couple have travelled the Mediterranean-climate regions of the world — from Crete and Greece to Spain, Portugal and the Atlas Mountains — studying how wild plants endure long, rainless summers. He is widely regarded as the leading voice in drought-tolerant planting.

On drought-resistant gardening

Filippi's central idea is deceptively simple: a garden in a dry climate should be built from plants that truly belong there, and it should ask for little or no irrigation at all. He looks to the garrigue and maquis — the wild Mediterranean scrub — as his model, favouring tough, aromatic, lean-soil plants that actively resent pampering and extra water. He argues that the lawn, made possible only by modern irrigation, has no real place in such a garden, and instead champions gravel gardens and undulating mounded beds composed for foliage, texture and form as much as for flower.

“In the future, water won't be available to us.”Olivier Filippi, Gardens Illustrated

Books: The Dry Gardening Handbook · Planting Design for Dry Gardens · Bringing the Mediterranean Into Your Garden.  Nursery: jardin-sec.com

Landscape Architect, Gardener & Writer · Greece

Jennifer Gay

Who she is

Jennifer Gay is a British-trained landscape architect who has lived and gardened in Greece since 1999. She studied horticulture at the Royal Horticultural Society's garden at Rosemoor and earned an MA in landscape architecture from the University of Sheffield, then gardened in southern England and in Jerusalem before moving to Greece to work at the Mediterranean Garden Society's garden at Sparoza, near Athens. She went on to help develop a botanical garden on the island of Kefalonia, and has been designing gardens across Greece — especially the Ionian and Aegean islands — ever since. She is also a long-standing gardening columnist and the author of Greece: Garden of the Gods.

On drought-resistant gardening

Gay's approach begins with reading a place closely — its soil, its exposure, its wind and water — and then choosing indigenous Greek and Mediterranean species suited to exactly those conditions, rather than forcing in plants destined to struggle. Her gardening is unflinchingly environmental: no artificial fertilisers or pesticides, with composting, mulching and natural balance doing the work instead. The result is a garden that shrugs off drought, wind, fire and erosion because it was built to belong, not to be propped up.

“It's better not to fight your conditions.”Jennifer Gay, Mediterranean Garden Society
